The Sovereignty of Self:
The Awakening in New England

Transcendentalism, born in the 1830s amid the intellectual heart of New England, teaches that truth and spiritual awakening are obtained through intuition and self-trust. It is a bold rejection of the "cold" intellectualism of Unitarianism, favoring instead a direct, vibrant spiritual experience that refuses to allow society to suppress the human spirit.

“Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist.” — Ralph Waldo Emerson

Inspired by European Romanticism, this movement seeks to awaken the "Over-soul"—that spiritual essence connecting all living beings. From the meetings of the Transcendental Club in 1836 to the radical simplicity of life in the woods, these thinkers are reshaping the American character.

Read of the Leaders and the Printed Word

The Vanguard of Thought

Ralph Waldo Emerson stands as the movement's architect, defining the principles of self-reliance. Beside him, Henry David Thoreau acts as the living example, choosing the simplicity of Walden Pond and the bravery of "Civil Disobedience" against unjust laws. Margaret Fuller, a formidable voice, has expanded these truths to champion the rights and education of women.

The Lasting Impact

This movement does not end with the individual. It has sparked a fire for environmental conservation and expanded into nearly every major reform movement of our age, from abolition to women's suffrage.
